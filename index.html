Deal structure is negotiable — traditional financing, seller financing, or sponsor equity. In practice, many sellers end up choosing to finance it themselves: it puts them in the lender's seat, creates a stream of passive income from the note payments, and can spread the capital gains tax over the life of the note instead of owing it all in the year of sale.

Why Sellers Often Choose to Finance

When you finance the sale, you become the bank — and the bank rarely loses. That's because the promise to pay isn't freestanding: an ordinary IOU is only as good as the person who signs it, but here it's backed by the property itself. You hold a deed of trust, the same security any lender requires, and if I default you don't need to sue me first — in most states, a deed of trust lets you foreclose without ever filing a court case, and you can start collecting rents immediately if the note assigns them to you. None of that depends on trusting me — it depends on paper that holds up in court.
